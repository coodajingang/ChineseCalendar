X indicates the month number before the leap month; MM-DD indicates the Julian date of the first day in the leap month. A blank cell means the date is the same as listed in the cell above.

447 Song 02-01 03-03 04-01 05-01 05-31 06-29 07-29 08-27 09-26 10-25 11-24 12-23 —
Northern Wei 12-23 13-22 10: 11-24

According to Index to Comprehensive Mirror to Aid in Governmance, the month 11 conjunction in 447 in the Northern Wei dynasty occurred on a jiǎ xū day (Dec. 23). However, in Compilation of Historical Calendars by Wāng Yuēzhēn, the month 12 conjunction was listed on a jiǎ xū day and is at odds with its statement that the winter solstice occurred on a jiǎ xū day in month 11. The month 12 conjunction on a jiǎ xū day is certainly a typo because a jiǎ xū day was 29 days (or 89 days) after a yǐ sì day, which was the leap month 10 conjunction date. So jiǎ xū day could only be the month 11 conjunction date. In 3500 Years of Calendars and Astronomical Phenomena by Zhāng Péiyú and Tables of Historical Lunar Conjunctions and Leap Months by Chén Yuán, the month 11 conjunction is mistakenly listed on Dec. 24. They were probably misled by Wāng's typo. The book A Sino-Western Calendar For Two Thousand Years (1-2000) by Hsueh Chung-San and Ouyang Yi correctly places the month 11 conjunction on Dec. 23. Surprisingly, the Chinese-Western calendar conversion website created by Academia Sinica in Taiwan, whose ancient calendar data are based on A Sino-Western Calendar For Two Thousand Years (1-2000), does not follow the book and mistakenly places the month 11 conjunction on Dec. 24.

Remark

A total of 11 calendars were used in this period covering 206 years. The Southern dynasties (Jin, Song, Qi, Liang and Chen) used the calendar based on the Jǐngchū astronomical system until 445 when it was replaced by the Yuánjiā system. The Yuánjiā system was replaced by the Dàmíng system in 510, which was used until the end of the Chen dynasty in 589.

In the north, the Later Qin state used the Sānjì system until the state was conquered by Jin in 417. The Northern Liang state didn't have its own calendar until 412 when an astronomer created the Xuánshǐ system and the calendar was used until the state was conquered by Northern Wei in 439. Northern Wei used the Jǐngchū system until 452 when it was replaced by Xuánshǐ system. The Xuánshǐ system was replaced by the Zhèngguāng system in 523. Northern Wei was divided into Eastern and Western Wei in 534. Eastern Wei continued to use the Zhèngguāng system until 540 when it was replaced by the Xīnghé system. In 550, Eastern Wei was replaced by Northern Qi and it used the Tiānbǎo system from 551 to the end of the dynasty in 557. Western Wei and its successor dynasty Northern Zhou continued to use the Zhèngguāng system until 559 when it was replaced by Míngkèràng system and then by Tiānhé system in 566. The Tiānhé system was replaced by the Dàxiàng system in 579. Sui dynasty took over Northern Zhou in 581 and it adopted the Kāihuáng system in 584. This system was in use until 597, which was after Sui unified China in 589 when it conquered the Chen dynasty.
